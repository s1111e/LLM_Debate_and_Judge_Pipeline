Multi-Agent Debate for StrategyQA

Evaluating whether multi-agent debate improves reasoning in large language models

Abstract Methodology Experiments Analysis Prompt Engineering Appendix

Abstract

This project investigates whether structured multi-agent debate can improve reasoning accuracy of large language models on the StrategyQA dataset. The system simulates a debate between two agents and a judge. We compare the debate pipeline with two baseline approaches: Direct Question Answering and Self Consistency. Results show that while debate can produce structured reasoning, it does not always outperform simpler baselines.

Methodology

The system consists of three agents: Debater A, Debater B, and a Judge. The debate process follows four stages:

  • Initial answers from both debaters
  • Multi-round debate
  • Judge evaluation
  • Final prediction extraction

Experiments

Experiments are conducted using 100 StrategyQA questions. Three reasoning strategies are compared.

Method Accuracy
Debate Pipeline 0.54
Direct QA 0.72
Self Consistency 0.72

Analysis

Debate transcripts reveal both success and failure cases. When one agent produces strong factual reasoning, the judge often selects the correct answer. However, debate may also propagate incorrect assumptions when both agents rely on flawed premises.

These findings align partially with the theoretical framework proposed by Irving et al., where debate exposes reasoning weaknesses through adversarial interaction.

Prompt Engineering

Role-based prompting is used to simulate debate. Debaters are instructed to provide YES/NO answers with reasoning and respond to opponent arguments. The judge evaluates the debate transcript and determines the final answer.
