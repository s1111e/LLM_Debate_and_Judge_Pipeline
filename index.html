Multi-Agent Debate for StrategyQA

Evaluating whether multi-agent debate improves reasoning in large language models

Abstract Methodology Experiments Analysis Prompt Engineering Appendix

Abstract

Large language models often struggle with complex reasoning tasks. Recent research suggests that structured debate between multiple agents may improve reasoning quality by exposing incorrect arguments. In this project we implement a multi-agent debate system consisting of two debaters and a judge. The system is evaluated on the StrategyQA dataset. We compare debate reasoning against two baseline methods: Direct QA and Self Consistency.

Our results show that although debate produces structured reasoning, it does not always outperform simpler baselines.

Methodology

System Architecture

The system consists of three agents:

  • Debater A
  • Debater B
  • Judge

The debate follows a four-phase reasoning pipeline.

Debate Protocol

Each debate begins with both agents producing an initial answer. Agents then respond to each other's arguments for several rounds. Finally, the judge evaluates the full transcript and determines the final answer.

Adaptive Stopping

To reduce unnecessary computation, the debate stops early when both agents converge to the same answer for two consecutive rounds.

Experiments

Experimental Setup

Experiments were conducted using 100 questions from the StrategyQA dataset. Three reasoning strategies were evaluated:

  • Debate Pipeline
  • Direct Question Answering
  • Self Consistency

Results

Method Accuracy
Debate Pipeline 0.54
Direct QA 0.72
Self Consistency 0.72

Analysis

Successful Debate Example

In several cases, debate successfully produced correct reasoning. For example, when answering whether kayaks are used at the summit of Mount Everest, one agent correctly explained that the summit contains no bodies of water, leading the judge to select the correct answer.

Failure Cases

Some debates failed when both agents relied on incorrect assumptions. In these cases the debate focused on irrelevant details, which caused the judge to rely on flawed reasoning.

Relation to Debate Theory

Irving et al. proposed debate as a method for improving reasoning transparency by allowing agents to challenge each other's arguments. However, our experiments suggest that debate may fail when both agents share the same incorrect premise.

Prompt Engineering

Role-based prompting was used to simulate debate. Each debater was instructed to provide a YES or NO answer with structured reasoning and respond to the opponent’s argument.

The judge was prompted to analyze the full debate transcript and select the final answer based on the strength of the arguments.

During development the prompts were iteratively refined to improve reasoning structure and enforce consistent output formats.
